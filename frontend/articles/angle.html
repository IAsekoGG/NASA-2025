Meteorite entry angle

How often and at what angles? If the flux of bodies in space is considered random, the most probable entry angle relative to the horizon is about 45°, and roughly half of events fall within ~30–60°. This result is widely used in planetary science and risk models.

How does angle affect crater shape and ejecta?

  • α ≳ 45° (moderate–steep entries). Craters are usually nearly circular, with ejecta that are more or less symmetric; asymmetry is modest.
  • α ≈ 15–45° (oblique entries). Craters remain predominantly circular, but ejecta become asymmetric: there is more “downrange” along the trajectory, while “uprange” a depletion zone forms — a hallmark of oblique impacts.
  • α ≲ 15° (very shallow entries). “Butterfly” ejecta patterns appear (two lobes with a minimum between them) and the structure shows greater elongation/ellipticity — the classic signature of a low angle in NASA/JPL imagery.
When can a body “skip” without striking the ground? At a few degrees above the horizon, earth-grazers are possible — meteoroids that skim the upper atmosphere and then exit back into space (like a stone “skipping” across water).

Ground effects also depend on angle. Shallow entries tend to shift energy and ejecta downrange and more often produce airbursts without a large crater; steeper entries transfer energy into the ground more efficiently and increase the chance of a distinct impact crater.
