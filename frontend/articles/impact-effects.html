Meteorite sizes

In practice, micrometeorites are classified primarily by diameter and by the degree of melting during atmospheric entry. Below is a concise guide.

Size classification

Category Diameter Where collected Features
IDP (interplanetary dust) < 50 μm Stratosphere Reference “smaller” class; these are not ground-collected micrometeorites
Micrometeorites (MM) 10 μm – 2 mm Antarctica, deserts, city rooftops The main share of extraterrestrial material reaching Earth’s surface
MM — unmelted tens–hundreds μm Same locations Preserve primary mineralogy
MM — scoriaceous (porous) tens–hundreds μm Same locations Partially melted, porous
MM — cosmic spherules ≈100–400 μm (common in urban collections) Rooftops, Antarctica Fully melted; wide variety of shapes and colors
Small meteorites/fragments 2 mm – 2 cm Strewn fields of witnessed falls; deserts, Antarctica Fresh fusion crust, often many fragments
Minor meteorites 2–5 cm Deserts, Antarctica, witnessed falls Most typical “finds”; masses from tens of grams up to ≈1 kg
Medium meteorites 5–20 cm Deserts, Antarctica Hundreds of grams to a few kilograms; common across catalogs
Large meteorites 20–100 cm Rare “large finds” Tens–hundreds of kg; sometimes fragments of crater-forming events
Record-size meteorites > 1 m Extremely rare Tons (e.g., Hoba ≈60 t; Willamette ≈15.5 t)
