Why the ground matters, too

Impact outcomes are determined not only by the bolide’s size and speed, but also by the nature of the target: its strength, density, porosity, and grain size. These factors control the crater’s shape, how much material is ejected, and how far it travels.

Rock

In hard bedrock, the impact first excavates a transient “bowl,” then fractures and spallation (detachment of thin slabs) build a larger final crater. Ejecta are energetic, the rim is well defined, and rays extend far — a rocky target “holds” the impact well and returns much of the energy outward.

Sand

A granular target behaves like a classic excavator: you get a rounded bowl with a broad carpet of ejecta around it. Most material comes from the near-surface layers, so the rays literally record the “fingerprint” of the surface. The edges slump more gently than in rock.

Highly porous materials

Here the impact mainly collapses pores — producing a “compaction crater.” Ejecta are few or nearly absent, move slowly, and fall back quickly. In the field this looks like a clean bowl with no extensive rays and only a soft rim.

The same bolide will leave a deeper, “harder” imprint with long rays in rock; in sand — a wider but gentler crater with a broad ejecta blanket; and in a super-porous target — a quiet impression with minimal scatter. One more detail: for oblique entries the rays shift downrange along the flight path — visible for all target types.
