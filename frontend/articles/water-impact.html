Tsunamis and “steam” plumes

For small and medium bodies (approximately <≈250 m), the expected tsunamis are generally modest: energy from an airburst couples weakly into the ocean surface, wave propagation is dispersive (shorter waves lose amplitude quickly), and on the continental shelf the Van Dorn effect further reduces it. Therefore, for typical scenarios the overall risk of large tsunamis and “steam” plumes is considered low.

The initial wave height near the source increases with the body’s size and speed (via a larger transient cavity) and is roughly limited by the local depth; a direct impact into deep water yields a larger initial wave than an atmospheric explosion. In practice, sub-kilometer objects in the ocean produce local waves from a few to tens of meters that quickly lose energy en route to shore, whereas kilometer-scale bodies can generate extreme values (scale reference — Chicxulub models with open-ocean waves >100 m near the impact zone). The continental shelf can “break” even a strong wave packet before it reaches the coastline.

“Steam” plumes

Noticeable vaporization effects are expected mainly for large and fast impacts: shock vaporization of water forms a rising steam–aerosol plume extending hundreds of kilometers. For sizes typical of planetary-defense scenarios (<≈250 m), these effects are usually minor.
