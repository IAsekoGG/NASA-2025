Why does a meteoroid fragment?

During entry, the dynamic pressure ≈ ½ρ v2 rises. When it exceeds the body’s actual strength (often reduced by cracks and porosity), the object undergoes progressive fragmentation: part of the mass becomes a cloud of fine particles, and part remains as discrete fragments. This behavior is captured by Fragment–Cloud-type models, which combine a particle cloud with individual fragments and account for different internal structures (including rubble-pile bodies).

Does this mitigate or worsen the effects?

Breakup at altitude lowers the likelihood of a large crater and a localized “point” catastrophe, because a significant fraction of the energy is released in the air. At the same time, it can strengthen the airburst: there exists an optimal burst height (Height-of-Burst) at which, for a given yield, the radius of hazardous overpressures at the surface is largest. Thus, fragmentation can reduce the cratering risk while increasing the area affected by the shock wave.

Breakup altitude & kinematics

The higher and faster the fragmentation occurs, the longer the fall “corridor” and the longer the strewn field; the mass distribution determines which pieces survive into the dark flight phase.

Fragment ballistics

Mass, density, and shape/drag coefficient set the downrange travel: heavier fragments go farther, lighter ones decelerate sooner.

Winds during dark flight

After the luminous phase fades, fragments fall at terminal speeds and become sensitive to wind profiles; crosswinds produce wider, often “banana-shaped” strewn fields.
