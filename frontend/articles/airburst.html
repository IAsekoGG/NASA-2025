Why does it explode in the atmosphere?

During entry, the dynamic pressure increases (≈ ½ρ v2). When it exceeds the body’s “effective” strength (weakened by cracks and porosity), the meteoroid fragments, its energy couples rapidly to the air, and an airburst occurs. For objects up to hundreds of meters, this scenario is typical; a scale example is the Chelyabinsk event: an explosion at ~20–23 km altitude produced a powerful shock wave without a ground impact.

Why consequences can be worse than a water or ground impact

An airburst efficiently converts energy into a broad ground-level blast with a large radius of overpressure. For a given yield there is a burst altitude at which the damage area is maximized (the optimal burst height). In Chelyabinsk, more than 1,600 people were injured — mostly by shattered glass — and thousands of buildings were damaged. NASA modeling (Cart3D/ATAP) indicates that a suitably “placed” airburst can affect a larger area of serious damage than a localized impact into hard ground, while coupling to the ocean generally converts energy poorly into far-field tsunami.

Airburst = a wide shock-wave footprint; the critical factors are energy, height of peak energy deposition, and entry angle.

Why the footprint is elliptical, not circular

For an oblique entry, the energy source moves along the trajectory, so the shock front forms anisotropically. NASA models describe this as an elliptical/“butterfly” footprint: the zone of maximum overpressures is oriented downrange along the flight path, with a cross-range decrease. This signature is consistently reproduced in high-fidelity simulations and observed in real events.
