What are meteorites made of?

According to NASA ARES, meteorites are traditionally divided into three major groups by material: stony, iron, and stony-iron. The division is based on texture and the content of silicates and metal (Fe–Ni).

Stony

The most common (>95% of witnessed falls). They include chondrites and achondrites.

  • Chondrites. Contain millimeter-sized “spheres” — chondrules — that formed ~4.5 billion years ago in the solar nebula. They preserve the earliest, “primitive” record of asteroid accretion. View 3D sample
  • Achondrites. Rocks without chondrules that underwent melting/differentiation; some originate from the Moon and Mars. They reveal volcanism and the “geology” of their parent bodies.
Example from NASA missions: The sample from asteroid Bennu (considered representative of the carbonaceous, “C-complex”) shows high contents of carbon and water — evidence that such bodies could have delivered the “building blocks of life” to early Earth. Water-bearing clay minerals are present.

Iron

Composed predominantly of iron–nickel metal; very dense. These are fragments of the cores of differentiated asteroids that cooled extraordinarily slowly; slices often show the Widmanstätten pattern. Such specimens directly reveal the internal structure of “minor planets” and the formation of their cores. View 3D sample

Stony-iron

Contain roughly equal amounts of metal and silicates; rare (<2%).

  • Pallasites. Olivine crystals in a Fe–Ni metal framework; likely sourced from the core–mantle boundary of small bodies.
  • Mesosiderites. Mixed “metal + basalt” breccias indicating asteroid collisions and layer mixing — offering a cross-section of disruption and mixing processes in the early Solar System.
